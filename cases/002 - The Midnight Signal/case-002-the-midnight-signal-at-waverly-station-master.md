# Locked Case File

## Public Premise

It is a rainy Friday evening in 1947. Waverly Station WVRL is hosting a live charity broadcast from its converted theater: music, comedy, sponsor spots, and Arthur Bell’s beloved midnight-style sign-off, though the show airs earlier for a local fundraiser.

Arthur Bell, owner and signature voice of Waverly Station, is heard over the air after the second break. Minutes later, he is found dead in the Record Library. Everyone insists they heard him speaking live. Someone at the station knows that “live” voice was not live at all.

## Victim

**Arthur Bell** — Founder and owner of WVRL. Warm public voice, exacting private manager, respected by listeners, feared by anyone with something to hide. He recently ordered a quiet audit of the charity broadcast account.

## Culprit

**Julian Rusk** — Program director. Polished, efficient, proud of his reputation for making every broadcast sound perfect. He had been diverting small amounts from the charity account, expecting to repay them before anyone noticed. Arthur discovered the discrepancy and planned to remove him and inform the charity board.

## True Solution Summary

Julian knew Arthur planned to expose him after the broadcast. Earlier that evening, Julian prepared two acetate recordings of Arthur’s voice using old rehearsal takes and carefully edited station phrases. During the broadcast, Julian created an excuse to send the chief engineer away from the control room, lured Arthur to the Record Library, killed him with the heavy chrome base from a lobby display microphone, and then used the recordings to create the illusion Arthur was still alive.

Julian’s plan depended on everyone trusting what they heard over the hallway speakers and broadcast monitors. His mistakes were small but fair: one pre-recorded segment used an outdated sponsor slogan, a fresh acetate disc left shavings near Turntable 2, and the broadcast log falsely marked a “live” microphone segment at a time when the control-room equipment showed playback.

## Method

Arthur was struck once with the heavy chrome base of an old display microphone. The scene must remain non-graphic. The base was wiped and returned to the lobby display case, but it retained a tiny new dent and a smear of dark polish from the Record Library floor. This is period-appropriate physical evidence.

## Motive

Arthur discovered that Julian had diverted money from the charity broadcast account. Arthur intended to confront him privately, remove him after the broadcast, and inform the charity board the next morning.

## Opportunity

Julian had access to the control room, recordings, broadcast logs, the Record Library, and the lobby display case. He also had enough authority to redirect staff during a live broadcast.

## Key Mistake

Julian’s fake “live” announcement used an old Brightway Soap sponsor line that had been replaced by a new card at 5:30 p.m. The old line remained only in the earlier rehearsal material Julian used to build the recording.

# Timeline

## True Timeline

**5:30 p.m.** — Sponsor representative Ruth Alden delivers revised Brightway Soap copy. The old slogan is replaced.

**6:10 p.m.** — Arthur Bell tells station bookkeeper Clara Finch by phone that the charity account “does not balance” and that he will speak with Julian after the show.

**6:40 p.m.** — Julian privately records and edits two acetate segments using rehearsal audio of Arthur’s voice and stock station phrases. He labels them as harmless station IDs.

**7:15 p.m.** — Staff gather before the broadcast. Arthur is tense but cordial. Julian appears unusually calm.

**8:00 p.m.** — The charity broadcast begins live.

**8:25 p.m.** — Arthur reads a sponsor line live from the corrected Brightway Soap card.

**8:31 p.m.** — Julian tells chief engineer Tom Beckett to check a rooftop antenna relay after reporting a fictitious signal flutter.

**8:34 p.m.** — Julian asks Arthur to step into the Record Library to discuss a “private timing problem” before Arthur’s next spoken segment.

**8:37 p.m.** — Arthur enters the Record Library with Julian.

**8:39 p.m.** — Arthur is killed. A shelf clock in the Record Library stops when it is knocked from a low shelf.

**8:41 p.m.** — Julian returns to the control room alone. He starts the first prepared acetate segment on Turntable 2.

**8:43 p.m.** — Staff and listeners hear Arthur’s voice over the broadcast monitors. They assume he is alive in Studio A.

**8:46 p.m.** — Julian marks the log as “Bell live mic — Studio A,” though Studio A’s microphone was not open.

**8:51 p.m.** — Second prepared segment plays. It includes the outdated Brightway Soap slogan.

**8:55 p.m.** — Tom returns from the rooftop and notices Turntable 2 is warmer than expected and has fresh acetate shavings nearby.

**9:02 p.m.** — Custodian Sam Ortiz notices the Record Library door is ajar.

**9:05 p.m.** — Arthur Bell is found deceased in the Record Library.

## Public / False Timeline

- Arthur was alive at 8:25 for the sponsor line.
- Arthur was supposedly heard live at 8:43 and 8:51.
- Julian claims Arthur must have died after 8:51.
- Most suspects build their alibis around hearing Arthur’s voice, not seeing Arthur himself.

# Suspect Roster

## 1. Julian Rusk — Program Director — Culprit

- Relationship to victim: Senior employee and creative manager.
- Personality: Controlled, polished, charming when watched, sharp when pressured.
- Public motive: Arthur planned to restructure the station and might demote him.
- Hidden secret: Diverted money from the charity broadcast account.
- Alibi claim: In the control room managing the live broadcast continuously from 8:30 to 8:55.
- True whereabouts: Left the control room briefly with Arthur, killed him in the Record Library, then returned and played prepared recordings.
- What he knows: The “live” later segments were recordings.
- What he hides: The charity money, the old sponsor-line recording, and the false log entry.
- Evidence pointing toward him: Access to recordings/logs, old sponsor line, fresh acetate, false control-room log, engineer sent away, knowledge of audit.
- Evidence pointing away from him: Staff heard Arthur’s voice while Julian appeared to be at the board.
- Questioning style: Smooth, impatient with “amateur theories,” overuses technical language to confuse simple questions.

## 2. Maribel Lane — Featured Singer

- Relationship to victim: Station star under contract.
- Personality: Warm in public, proud, emotionally expressive.
- Public motive: Arthur refused to release her from an exclusive contract.
- Hidden secret: She had already accepted an unsigned offer from a larger station.
- Alibi claim: In the Green Room preparing sheet music, then sang at 8:49.
- True whereabouts: In the Green Room from 8:32 to 8:47 with Eddie briefly nearby; in Studio B by 8:48.
- What she knows: Arthur had been unusually stern that evening.
- What she hides: The unsigned offer letter.
- Evidence pointing toward her: Contract dispute, torn letter, tense conversation with Arthur.
- Evidence pointing away: She was not near the Record Library during the crucial earlier window; her performance prep is supported by sheet-music markings and Eddie’s sighting.
- Questioning style: Defensive at first, becomes cooperative if treated respectfully.

## 3. Tom Beckett — Chief Engineer

- Relationship to victim: Longtime technical employee.
- Personality: Practical, blunt, loyal to equipment and procedure.
- Public motive: Arthur rejected his expensive safety upgrades.
- Hidden secret: He had threatened to quit after Arthur cut the engineering budget.
- Alibi claim: On the rooftop checking antenna equipment from 8:32 to 8:54.
- True whereabouts: On the rooftop for most of the key window, sent there by Julian.
- What he knows: There was no real signal flutter; Turntable 2 was warm when he returned.
- What he hides: He was angry enough to write a resignation note.
- Evidence pointing toward him: Technical knowledge, argument with Arthur, access to equipment.
- Evidence pointing away: Rooftop dust/tar on shoes, rooftop door log, and his observation about Turntable 2 help reveal Julian’s trick.
- Questioning style: Gruff, factual, easily annoyed by theatrics.

## 4. Ruth Alden — Sponsor Representative

- Relationship to victim: Advertising partner for Brightway Soap.
- Personality: Precise, businesslike, image-conscious.
- Public motive: Arthur threatened to reject a lucrative sponsorship due to misleading claims.
- Hidden secret: She changed sponsor copy at 5:30 p.m. under pressure from her company.
- Alibi claim: In the lobby and sponsor office reviewing ad cards from 8:30 to 8:55.
- True whereabouts: Moving between the sponsor office and lobby; not near the Record Library at 8:37–8:41.
- What she knows: The 8:51 sponsor line was wrong; Arthur would not have read that outdated wording live.
- What she hides: Her company’s embarrassing old slogan change.
- Evidence pointing toward her: Sponsor dispute, revised ad card, anxiety about copy.
- Evidence pointing away: She becomes key to proving the recorded segment was not live.
- Questioning style: Cautious, corrects details, hates being publicly embarrassed.

## 5. Eddie Vale — Junior Announcer

- Relationship to victim: Arthur’s nephew and junior announcer.
- Personality: Eager, insecure, sincere.
- Public motive: He wanted Arthur to promote him to a prime announcing slot.
- Hidden secret: He had been practicing Arthur’s sign-off voice privately, hoping to impress him.
- Alibi claim: Near the Green Room and Studio B during the key period.
- True whereabouts: Passed the Green Room at 8:36, saw Maribel inside, then waited near Studio B.
- What he knows: He saw Julian with a flat acetate sleeve earlier but assumed it was routine.
- What he hides: His imitation practice, which makes him fear suspicion.
- Evidence pointing toward him: Voice imitation, ambition, family tension.
- Evidence pointing away: His imitation does not match the broadcast recording; he helps place Maribel away from the Record Library.
- Questioning style: Nervous, tries too hard, blurts out useful details if reassured.

# Clue Logic Map

## 1. False Assumption
The false assumption is that Arthur was alive because his voice was heard at 8:43 and 8:51.

## 2. Timeline Detail That Matters
Arthur was seen alive at 8:25. The shelf clock stopped at 8:39. The later “live” voice segments can be proven to be recordings.

## 3. Culprit Alibi Break
Julian claims he was managing a live microphone segment, but the control room wax-pencil board and Turntable 2 evidence show playback was used. Tom’s rooftop errand also left Julian alone with the board.

## 4. Method / Timeline Clues
The fresh acetate shavings, warm Turntable 2, old sponsor line, and false log entry prove a recording was used to fake Arthur’s continued presence.

## 5. Motive Clues
Arthur’s office note “J.R. — charity ledger — after broadcast. No delay” plus Clara’s statement about copied approval initials point toward Julian’s financial misconduct.

## 6. Opportunity Clues
Julian controlled the broadcast, sent Tom away, had access to acetate recordings, and could lure Arthur privately under the excuse of show timing.

## 7. Why Julian Fits All Facts
Only Julian had the combined motive, technical access, authority to redirect Tom, access to the old sponsor line, ability to falsify the log, and opportunity to play the recordings.

## 8. Why Others Are Not Guilty
- Maribel had a motive but lacks the technical access and is placed away from the Record Library.
- Tom had technical access but was sent to the rooftop and provides evidence exposing the trick.
- Ruth had sponsor conflict but her revised card proves the segment was pre-recorded.
- Eddie had voice-imitation skills but lacked control-room access and did not know the old sponsor change.

# Gameplay Rules

- Reveal clues when the player reasonably investigates the right person, place, object, or contradiction.
- Do not withhold essential clues behind overly exact wording.
- If a clue is essential, allow alternate discovery through inspection, questioning, or comparison.
- Keep suspects in character.
- Do not reveal hidden facts unless discovered, logically inferred, or included in the final reveal.
- Do not start the final reveal unless the player solves the case, asks for it, or makes a formal accusation.

---

# Character Questioning Rules

Suspects should answer according to what they know and hide. They may be evasive, embarrassed, defensive, or mistaken, but they should not lie in impossible ways. The culprit may lie to preserve the false timeline, but should slip under pressure through inconsistencies.

---

# Hint Rules

When the player asks for a hint:

1. Start with a gentle nudge.
2. If asked again, point toward a useful location or comparison.
3. If asked again, identify a contradiction.
4. Only give a near-solution hint if the player directly asks for stronger help.

Example hint path:

- Gentle: “Think about what everyone heard versus what anyone actually saw.”
- Stronger: “The 8:51 sponsor line may not belong to the same moment everyone thinks it does.”
- Strongest: “Compare the sponsor copy, Turntable 2, and Julian’s broadcast log.”

---

# Formal Accusation Stage

When the player makes an accusation, ask them to provide:

1. Who did it?
2. Why?
3. How?
4. Which clues prove it?

Evaluate fairly.

- If correct and sufficiently supported, proceed to the final reveal.
- If correct but under-supported, say they are close and ask for more evidence.
- If incorrect, explain which parts of the evidence do not fit without revealing the true culprit unless the player asks for the reveal.

---

# Ending the Game

The final reveal should explain:

- Arthur died before the later voice segments.
- Julian used recordings to fake a live broadcast.
- The old sponsor line exposed the recording.
- Turntable 2, acetate shavings, and the false log broke Julian’s alibi.
- Arthur’s ledger note established motive.
- Each innocent suspect’s suspicious behavior had a separate explanation.

End with a clean conclusion and optional invitation to replay on a higher difficulty.
